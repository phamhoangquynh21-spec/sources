01
10 marks

Business Problem Identification

GreenSprout Foods Pty Ltd, a Toowoomba-based food manufacturer with ~$4.5M annual revenue, faced a critical operational problem during its Audit Committee meeting: inventory management inefficiency driven by unpredictable demand fluctuations (GreenSprout Foods, 2026).

Operating across supermarkets, health food stores, farmers' markets, and an online store simultaneously creates fragmented, hard-to-track demand signals. Without a unified view, GreenSprout cannot distinguish seasonal peaks from structural growth — leading directly to the bottlenecks, overstocking, and revenue losses identified in the quarterly performance review.

"Production bottlenecks during peak demand periods, seasonal inventory fluctuations, and expanding online sales all point to a fundamental inability to predict and respond to real-time consumer demand."

📦
Bottlenecks
Manufacturing can't scale fast enough for peak periods
📊
Swings
Seasonal over- and under-stocking wastes perishables
🛒
Multi-Channel
Five channels, no unified demand signal

15 marks

How It Solves the Problem

The platform replaces reactive stock management with proactive, data-driven forecasting. Implementation follows three key phases:

01
Data Integration Across All Channels
Consolidates sales data from all GreenSprout channels into one system, eliminating information silos and enabling a complete real-time operational picture (Chopra & Meindl, 2022).
02
Predictive Seasonal Modelling
ML models trained on GreenSprout's historical patterns predict demand spikes weeks in advance, allowing proactive production and ingredient scheduling — eliminating reactive bottlenecks.
03
Automated Replenishment & Alert Systems
When forecast models detect critical stock thresholds, the system auto-generates supplier orders and alerts the operations team — preventing both stockouts and overproduction.
04
30 marks

Professional, Societal & Ethical Implications

AI inventory management shifts staff roles from manual stock counting to higher-value analytical work — interpreting forecasts, managing suppliers, and optimising production scheduling (Schwab, 2017).

Decision-making becomes data-driven, reducing costly human errors during peak periods. However, new competencies in AI literacy and data interpretation are required — demanding investment in professional development (World Economic Forum, 2023).

This transition must be managed carefully to avoid employee disengagement or resistance to the change in working practices.

Role Evolution
Manual tasks give way to analytical oversight — a professionally enriching shift that increases job value and complexity.
Upskilling Needed
AI literacy, data interpretation, and system management become essential new competencies for all operations staff.
Better Decisions
AI-augmented decisions are faster, more consistent, and grounded in real data — reducing risk and improving outcomes.

Better inventory management reduces food waste — Australia wastes ~7.6 million tonnes of food annually (FIAL, 2021). Accurate forecasting directly reduces overproduction and perishable spoilage.

For customers, improved inventory means consistent product availability and faster online fulfilment. However, smaller retail partners may lack infrastructure to integrate with AI supply chain tools (Brynjolfsson & McAfee, 2014).

Consumer data privacy is also a societal concern — purchase data used in forecasting models must comply with the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) to maintain public trust.

Food Waste Reduction
Precise forecasting eliminates systematic overproduction of perishables, reducing GreenSprout's environmental footprint.
Digital Equity
Smaller regional retail partners may be excluded if supply chain digitisation creates barriers for businesses without advanced systems.
Consumer Trust
Transparent data practices and consistent availability build long-term brand loyalty in the health food market.

Algorithmic transparency is a core concern — AI forecasting models are often "black boxes" whose decision logic is not easily interpretable (Diakopoulos, 2016). If a model errs, managers must be able to understand why.

Data bias presents another risk. If training data excludes Toowoomba's unique regional market characteristics, the model may systematically under-stock locally popular products while over-prioritising national trends.

Responsible data use requires that customer purchase histories are collected with consent, stored securely, and never used beyond stated purposes (OAIC, 2023).

Transparency
AI must be explainable — dashboards should surface the key factors driving each forecast so managers can audit the reasoning.
Bias Risk
Models must be trained on regionally representative data reflecting Toowoomba's unique market, not only national patterns.
Data Consent
Consumer data requires opt-in consent and full compliance with the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and Australian Privacy Principles.
05
15 marks

Risk Mitigation Strategies

🎓
Staff Training & Phased Change Management
Structured training in AI literacy and data interpretation. Phased rollout starting with one product category limits operational risk during transition (Schwab, 2017).
🔍
Explainable AI & Human Approval Gates
Require explainable AI outputs — dashboards showing forecast-driving factors. Managers must approve all AI-generated orders above a financial threshold before execution.
🔒
Data Protection & Privacy Compliance
Data governance policy aligned with Privacy Act 1988 (Cth). Privacy Impact Assessment before deployment. All customer data anonymised and consent-based (OAIC, 2023).
🤝
Inclusive Design for Partner Retailers
Provide smaller retail partners with simple integration pathways or manual alternatives, preserving GreenSprout's regional distribution network.
🔄
Quarterly Model Auditing
Quarterly audits comparing predictions vs. actual outcomes. Retrain with updated local Toowoomba market data to continuously reduce regional bias.
